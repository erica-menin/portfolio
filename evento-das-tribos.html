Evento das Tribos

How I developed a Curated Event Microsite design in 4 days. My 6th project at Ironhack Bootcamp.

Project Brief

Developing an event microsite in 4 days – individually. The idea was to go through the whole process, from research to test – so we could get the feeling on how the Design Sprint process works and validate all things we’ve learned so far — from time management to more specific skills.

The Challenge

To create an event microsite for a client of our own choice — that currently has a website that needs improvement or that doesn’t have a website at all.

The Event

Encontro das Tribos (Tribe’s Encounter in Portuguese) is a Brazilian daily radio show that became a music festival. The event brings different artists from the national scene together.

The radio show is transmitted in more than 360 cities in the state of São Paulo, and has more than 1 million listeners through the whole country, according to IBOPE data.

Current Scenario

Due to the COVID-19 situation, the event had to be postponed. The user can only get information about the concert from a Facebook page and the ticket provider website.

Since it’s the Facebook Page has a timeline format, sometimes users miss important information and the organisation finds themselves answering the same questions, over and over again.

That’s how the Facebook page looks like

User Research

To understand which questions the users are making and what they are looking for on a music event microsite, other than just checking the Facebook page, I made some research and came to some conclusions. Basically, since the event was postponed due to the CoronaVirus, users are confused about the confirmed attractions.

Funny fact: according to the feedback I got from a survey, the main things users will check out on a website are first lineup and then price. But how to be sure about the lineup now?

I also asked people more than just what is more relevant when checking an event website, but also what they consider it would be nice to have in there, to make it more appealing.

Empathy Map

After gathering research data, I was able to fill up the Empathy Map Canvas, so I could start to define the target audience. This was the basis to develop a User Persona later on.

Meet Raquel, our new customer

Raquel loves music concerts and tries to attend as many as she can! She sees this kind of festival as more than just an event. For her, it’s the perfect place with the perfect vibe to create and share memories with friends.

She heard about Evento das Tribos this year, and due to the change of date, she’s now able to go! She’s super excited but she is not sure about the whole lineup shared on the Facebook page, which makes her have second thoughts about the event and the organisation.

Raquel can't be sure about the lineup or the news about the event on their Facebook page

Brainstorming

According to Raquel’s needs, there’s something we can ask ourselves:

“How might we make users feel better with information we DO have and keep them informed and entertained?”

To answer that I tried to define what we must, should, and could have in the microsite:

  • First of all, we can make the information the user needs more visible, which in this case would be the lineup
  • Second, we can motivate them to subscribe to our newsletter so they receive the fresh news and keep themselves updated
  • And third, we can keep them entertained with playlists with songs from the artists that are definitely playing at the festival, something they would like according to the survey

Sitemap

After that, I came up with a simple structure of the website, which would be a landing page to go straight to the point. This guided me to create the first low-fi wireframes later on.

Visual Competitors Analysis – More research

Time to start to think about design!

But what’s working out there? I had a look at how the competitors’ websites were to compare them and check how they behave. Based on that, I’ve decided to keep the colourful mood, something that seems to be really working today.

Moodboard

Based on that, I created a moodboard with the goal to translate these words in a visual way. To validate it, I tested it with potential users, asking them the same question, and they seemed to match very well!

The same words like energetic, vibrant and cheerful came up again. That was great! Setting up the mood led me to the next step.

Style Tiles

To finally start working on the design, I came up with two versions of Style Tiles, to test a few typefaces and interface elements — always keeping the essence of the brand in mind.

High Fidelity Screens

I created three different screens to be tested with users before really deciding which direction to follow.

After a few iterations, I combined different options to get the to the final design style. This time I ran a desirability test.

Prototype

Style Guide

With the first part ready, I was able to create a mini Style Guide. That would help me — and eventually other designers that could be involved in the project — to keep the consistency when developing new pages.

New pages? Yes!

Here is a first impression on how the page would look like during and after the event.

  • During the event: the user would have all the information about the time and stages that each artist would play, so they are still updated about everything that is going on.They would also be encouraged to share pictures, videos, tweets or whatever they liked on social media, through an event hashtag.
  • After the event: a thank you (obrigado!) at the home screen, still encouraging the use of the hashtag; a quick message saying “see you next year” followed by lots of pictures and videos from the edition — right, Raquel? If you remember her, she loves to create and share memories with friends, and that is one of the ways she could do that.
